# Vibe Creating — the philosophy

> English port of ByteDance / Volcengine's *Vibe Creating Practice Handbook*. See [NOTICE](../NOTICE) for attribution.

## The smarter the model, the simpler creation

The release of **Seedance 2.0** moved AI video generation into a new phase. The model supports versatile reference, and shows striking ability in **physical realism, long-video consistency, and aesthetics** — letting creators turn inspiration into footage with unprecedented efficiency.

As creators applied it to real work, a pattern became clear:

> **Very often, even without breaking every action into the prompt, and without piling on professional camera jargon, the model still produces footage that is beautiful and highly complete.**

"Getting good footage, efficiently" turns out to be the natural result of a leap in model capability. The model's role in creation is quietly changing: it's no longer just a **tool** that passively executes instructions — it's becoming a **partner** that understands you and understands creation.

Of course, in commercial work with strict delivery standards or industrialized pipelines, extremely precise high-end prompts remain an indispensable professional instrument. But in the much broader space of **creative exploration and narrative expression**, the model-as-smart-partner gives us a freer way to work: first focus on **telling the story well**, then layer on precise control only where it's needed.

## Let creation return to expression

In the past, we worked hard to adapt to the model's way of expressing — instead of the model serving the creator's expression. **Being "reverse-trained" by the model is a massive drain on a creator's desire to express.**

A new way of collaborating with the model gives rise to a new creative idea, which we call **Vibe Creating**. Its claim is simple: **let creation return to expression itself.**

- A creator's energy should refocus on what truly matters: **telling the story, returning to intent.**
- When industrialized delivery isn't required, set aside the elaborate execution language, the precise parameter control, and the shot-by-shot scripts — **trust the model, give up over-control.**

### Pillar 1 — Focus on Story

**A full, complete story is the starting point of creation.** When the creator describes a scene in vivid, image-rich language — a character's situation, the texture of the air, the flow of emotion — the model behaves like a highly perceptive cinematographer, finding the right framing, light, and rhythm on its own.

*Shooting list vs. story expression:* focus on getting the rise-and-fall of the story right, and good images surface naturally. Under Vibe Creating, the story in the frame stands out more, and conveys the creator's core intent more faithfully than a list of camera moves would.

### Pillar 2 — Trust the Model

The other angle of Vibe Creating: **try giving the model a little more room.** Stop prescribing the focal length, composition rules, and movement parameters frame by frame. When you hand the elaborate execution over to the model, its free play often exceeds expectations — smoother transitions, more natural motion, a more elevated, cinematic color and texture.

**But giving up over-control doesn't mean deleting all camera language.** The key is to let the shot *serve the expression*:

- **Weaken or delete** the "tell the system how to shoot" technical vocabulary — focal-length params, camera-position jargon, exact movement values, DoF / aperture numbers — pure execution-layer instructions.
- **Keep and translate** the shot *intent* that conveys "how the viewer should feel" — e.g. turn "slow dolly-in" into "the gaze slowly closes in, building a sense of pressure."

## The prompt principle — give just the right amount of information

"Tell the story" and "don't over-control" do **not** mean shorter is always better. An empty concept with no situation or action still yields thin footage. Vibe Creating isn't avoiding *more* — it's avoiding the **ineffective more**; and it isn't adding *technical detail* — it's adding **the information that actually makes the frame stand up.**

A high-quality prompt usually carries four layers of information:

| Layer | What it is | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Visual anchor** | the most prominent subject or element | "an old man in a worn cotton coat", "a rainy-night street under neon" |
| **Action / state** | what the subject is doing or its state | "slowly turns to face the camera", "rain streaming down the glass" |
| **Local tonality** | light, color, rhythm — the texture of the frame | "warm amber backlight", "the slight sway of a handheld shot" |
| **Video theme** | where this clip is used + overall style | "a short film about goodbye", "a cyberpunk-style game CG" |

In most scenarios the model can produce a logically complete frame from **minimal** information — but the more complete the information, the more precisely the model grasps the creator's core intent.

## When Vibe Creating fits — and when it doesn't

Vibe Creating is **better at** expression centered on atmosphere, emotion, narrative feel, and visual association: the creator hands the "feeling" and "intent" to the model, then explores the visualization together.

In scenarios with explicit **industrial delivery standards** — precise dialogue sync, strict shot-by-shot execution, clear UI operation demos, strong causal step-by-step instructions — precise control is still the more reliable choice.

So this isn't about defining the one correct way to write. It's an invitation to see and try a new possibility: **in the right scenario, use Vibe Creating to unlock the model's potential.**
